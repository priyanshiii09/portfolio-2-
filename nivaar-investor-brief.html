Nivaar

Personalised physiotherapy and nutrition, delivered to the doorsteps of India's elderly — before pain becomes disability, and disability becomes dependence.

India is on the brink of becoming an old country. By 2050, one in every five Indians will be aged 60 or above — nearly 347 million people1. Almost half of them already live with knee osteoarthritis2, three-quarters with Vitamin D deficiency3, and yet fewer than 5% of our primary health centres offer geriatric services4.

NIVAAR is a community-rooted service that closes this gap — bringing trained physiotherapists and nutritionists into elders' homes, building motivation through community, and turning healthcare from a hospital visit into a daily, dignified habit.

01 The Quiet Crisis

India is ageing faster than its healthcare system can keep up.

Three demographic and clinical realities are converging silently. They are not yet a headline. They are about to become one.

The data below is drawn from peer-reviewed Indian medical literature, the UNFPA India Ageing Report, the Longitudinal Ageing Study in India (LASI), and the Ministry of Health & Family Welfare.

347M

Indians will be aged 60+ by 2050 — up from 149M in 2022.

That is a 134% increase in one generation. Those aged 80+ will grow by 279%, a "predominance of widowed and highly dependent very old women."1

SOURCE · UNFPA India Ageing Report, 2023
47%

Pooled prevalence of knee osteoarthritis among Indian elders.

A meta-analysis of 14 community studies (5,029 participants) found nearly half of India's elderly live with knee OA — with a rural Haryana study finding the figure as high as 64%.2

SOURCE · J. Family Medicine & Primary Care, 2025
53.5%

Of older Indian adults live with at least one musculoskeletal disorder.

A nationally representative survey of 28,436 participants found MSD prevalence rises to 60.4% in those over 60. Joint pain (41.9%) is more common than back pain (32.6%).5

SOURCE · LASI / NCBI Population Study
<5%

Of India's Primary Health Centres offer geriatric OPD services.

Only 4–14% of rural elders are even aware of geriatric welfare services available to them, and just 11% have any form of pension to pay for private care.4

SOURCE · MoHFW Survey · PRB India
75.7%

Of older Indians have insufficient Vitamin D. 42% are Vitamin B12 deficient.

27% of those aged 60+ are underweight; another 22% are overweight or obese — a dual nutritional burden that directly worsens bone, joint, and cognitive decline.3

SOURCE · Observer Research Foundation · ORF Policy Brief
01.b The Three Gaps

Each of these alone is solvable. The damage happens because they compound.

GAP 01 · Access

Care exists, but not where elders live.

Geriatric clinics cluster in tertiary urban hospitals. Mobility-limited elders cannot reach them. Home-care exists, but is fragmented, expensive, and concentrated among India's top 1%.

GAP 02 · Integration

Physio, nutrition, and mental health are sold separately.

An orthopedic visit treats the knee. A dietitian treats the deficiency. Nobody treats the elder — the person, with all three needs intertwined and reinforcing one another.

GAP 03 · Motivation

Even when care is prescribed, adherence collapses.

Exercises are taught once and forgotten. Diet plans gather dust. Without a system of community accountability and small daily wins, prescriptions become paperwork.

04 The Insight

The real gap isn't care. It's continuity.

Existing services in India hand elders a prescription, a diet chart, and a polite goodbye. NIVAAR's research surfaced four behavioural principles — the missing operating system of preventive eldercare. Every component of our service is engineered against these four.

PRINCIPLE 01

Awareness

Most elders do not know what is wrong, or that it is preventable. Education is the first dose.

PRINCIPLE 02

Motivation

In Indian culture, behaviour change happens in groups, not in clinics. Community is the lever.

PRINCIPLE 03

Consistency

Small daily wins compound. A weekly tick on a wall calendar matters more than a quarterly check-up.

PRINCIPLE 04

Active Involvement

Elders are not patients. They are participants. They choose the pathway. They invite their neighbours.

05 The Solution
Introducing
Nivaar

A door-to-door, personalised physiotherapy and nutrition service for India's elderly — bound together by a calendar on the wall, a weekly phone call, and a community that walks together.

STEP / 01
A trusted way in.

Most elders won't download an app. So NIVAAR begins with a phone call — from a family member, an NGO, or a community partner. Registration takes a name, an age, and an address. Nothing more.

Low-tech onboarding · trust-first
STEP / 02
A home visit, not a hospital visit.

A NIVAAR field officer visits, verifies basic ID, and a physiotherapist and nutritionist arrive within the week. They listen first, prescribe second. The elder co-designs their pathway from two or three options — vegetarian, time-constrained, budget-sensitive.

In-home · personalised · co-designed
STEP / 03
A calendar on the wall.

The elder receives a physical "Dietary Guide" — a wall-mounted board with foods coded by frequency (daily, weekly, monthly), a velcro-attached monthly calendar, and stickers to mark off completed sessions. Analog. Visible. Sharable with grandchildren.

Physical artefact · zero learning curve
STEP / 04
A rhythm of weekly check-ins.

The nutritionist calls weekly. The physiotherapist visits as prescribed (typically twice a month). Progress is logged. Plans are adjusted. The elder feels seen, not surveilled.

Continuous · adaptive · human
STEP / 05
A community grows around them.

Once a neighbourhood reaches a benchmark of subscribers, NIVAAR seeds community sessions — group walks, gardening days, shared exercise circles — funded in part by referrals. Motivation moves from individual to collective.

Network effect · cultural fit · group accountability
STEP / 06
A path that rewards growth.

Communities progress through Bronze, Silver, and Gold tiers — unlocking shared garden plots, communal meeting spaces, and partnerships with welfare associations and NGOs. The service is designed to become self-sustaining at the neighbourhood level.

Gamified at the community level · cooperative · scalable

07 The Market & The Moment

A market that is large, growing, and policy-supported.

The Indian eldercare market is not a future opportunity — it is a present one, growing at demographic speed. Multiple Government of India schemes have created favourable tailwinds for public-private partnerships in this space.

TAM Total Indian elderly population (60+), 2025 ~155 M
SAM Urban & peri-urban elders with family-paid healthcare capacity ~30 M
SOM (5yr) Reachable target through pilot → multi-city expansion ~250 K
Of the 155M Indian elders, ~73M live with knee OA2, and an estimated 50M would benefit immediately from integrated physio-nutrition intervention. We size our 5-year addressable opportunity conservatively against urban-clustered, family-supported segments.

Policy & structural tailwinds

01
AB-PMJAY for 70+ Seniors (Oct 2024)
₹5 lakh annual coverage extended to ~6 crore senior citizens regardless of income — a public payer ready for integrated preventive services.6
02
SAGE Portal & Silver Economy Initiative
Government equity support of up to ₹1 crore per eldercare startup via IFCI. Explicit signal that the state seeks credible private partners.6
03
NPHCE (National Programme for Healthcare of Elderly)
Mandate to extend geriatric services to district and community levels — under-resourced and actively seeking delivery partners.
04
CSR Eldercare Allocations
Schedule VII of the Companies Act, 2013 explicitly includes "measures for the benefit of senior citizens" — unlocking corporate CSR funding pools.

09 What We Will Measure

Impact, on three honest dimensions.

CLINICAL

WOMAC pain & function scores

Standardised orthopedic score, measured at enrolment, 3 months, 6 months, 12 months.

CLINICAL

Nutritional markers

BMI, mid-upper-arm circumference, MNA-SF scores; biochemical panels (Vit D, B12, Hb) for a sample.

CLINICAL

Functional independence (ADL)

Katz / Lawton scores. Number of activities of daily living an elder can perform unassisted.

BEHAVIOURAL

Adherence rate

% of prescribed weekly exercises & dietary inputs sustained over 30, 60, 90, 180 days.

BEHAVIOURAL

Loneliness & mood (UCLA-3, GDS-15)

Short-form geriatric depression and loneliness scales, measured pre- and post-community formation.

BEHAVIOURAL

Net Promoter (Family)

Would the elder's children recommend NIVAAR to another family? The market signal that matters most.

SYSTEMIC

Cost-per-elder-month

Total program cost divided by active elder-months. Trended over time as communities self-organise.

SYSTEMIC

Community formation rate

Months to seed a self-sustaining neighbourhood community; % of new enrolment via referral.

SYSTEMIC

Avoided hospitalisations

Cost-of-care averted through preventive intervention — the metric that justifies public payment.

11 The Stakeholder Map

A service of this kind cannot exist alone.

NIVAAR is intentionally designed as a multi-stakeholder system. Each actor below has a clearly defined role, value proposition, and incentive to participate.

PRIMARY USER

The Elder

Receives personalised, dignified care at home. Co-designs their own pathway.

DECISION-MAKER

The Adult Child

The buyer in most urban households. Pays peace-of-mind, receives transparency.

PROVIDER

Physiotherapists & Nutritionists

Stable employment, structured caseload, ongoing training. Trained to NIVAAR protocols.

PROVIDER

Field Officers / Coordinators

Hired locally. Cultural bridge between the elder and the clinical system.

CHANNEL

NGOs & Welfare Associations

Referral source, community-trust anchor, last-mile distribution at city periphery.

PAYER

Government (NPHCE / AB-PMJAY)

Pays for outcomes in low-income geographies; receives a credible preventive-care partner.

PAYER

Corporate CSR

Funds onboarding & community infrastructure; receives measurable, story-rich impact reporting.

PAYER

Impact Investors

Provides patient growth capital; receives a defensible, replicable model with social and financial returns.

ADVISORS

Clinicians & Academics

Co-design protocols, validate clinical rigour, co-publish peer-reviewed evaluations.

13 The Ask

What we need, and what we will deliver in return.

NIVAAR is at the stage where field research has converged on a sharp, validated service concept. We are seeking partners across three distinct categories to move from prototype to a measurable, year-one pilot — with full transparency on outcomes, costs, and lessons.

CATEGORY A

Pilot Funding

Seed and Series-A-stage capital from impact investors and family offices. Supports the 12-month pilot — team hiring, clinical infrastructure, and a rigorous M&E framework. In return: equity / convertible, board observer rights, and a defensible thesis in India's silver economy.

CATEGORY B

CSR & Grant Partners

Multi-year CSR allocations under the Companies Act Schedule VII. Funds community-infrastructure layers — calendars, gardens, communal spaces — that subscriber revenue cannot. In return: co-branded reach in priority neighbourhoods, audited impact reporting, and rich narrative for ESG disclosure.

CATEGORY C

Government & NGO Allies

MoUs with NPHCE district units, state health departments, and welfare-association networks. Co-deliver preventive eldercare in priority geographies. In return: a credible private partner with transparent outcome data and zero claim on government IP.
