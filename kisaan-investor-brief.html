Kisaan

A proposal to lift small farmers out of the middleman trap — through cooperative ownership, organic practice, and a wall-mounted information board in every village.

The 90-Second Brief

India has 146 million operational farm holdings. 86% of them are smaller than two hectares.1 Their owners feed the country — and lose nearly two-thirds of every consumer rupee to middlemen.

For the Indian dairy farmer, that ratio is the opposite. Amul returns 75–85% of consumer price to its 3.6 million producers.2 The difference is not crop — it is structure. It is the cooperative.

Project KISAAN proposes to replicate that structure for the grain farmer in Khimsepur, Farrukhabad — layered with organic methods supported by the Government of India's Paramparagat Krishi Vikas Yojana.3 One village. One hundred farms. A proof point we can take national.

I Chapter One — The Quiet Crisis FOL. 02

India still eats by the hand of its smallest farmers.

Two facts hold up Indian agriculture, and they pull in opposite directions. The first: smallholders feed the country. The second: smallholders cannot afford to be the people doing it.

Every figure on the following pages is drawn from the Agricultural Census, the NSO Situation Assessment Survey, the National Crime Records Bureau, the UN Food and Agriculture Organization, and peer-reviewed Indian agricultural economics.

86.2%

Of all Indian farmers are small or marginal — owning less than two hectares of land.

Together, they own just 47.3% of cropland. Average holding size has shrunk from 2.28 ha in 1970 to 1.08 ha today, and for marginal farmers it is only 0.38 ha.1

SOURCE · Agriculture Census 2015-16 · NSO 77th Round
30%

The share of consumer price that reaches the small fruit, vegetable, and grain farmer.

The dairy farmer, by contrast, receives 75–85%, thanks to seven decades of cooperative milk procurement. The crop is different. The structure is different. The result is different.2

SOURCE · NDDB · ICRIER (Gulati & Das) · GCMMF Annual Report FY25
10,786

Indians in the farming sector died by suicide in 2023 alone.

That is one farmer or farm labourer every 49 minutes. NCRB data records more than 3.9 lakh such deaths between 1995 and 2023 — an average of 13,600 per year.4

SOURCE · NCRB Accidental Deaths & Suicides 2023
1%

Of marginal Indian farmers own a tractor. Only 14% have access to formal credit.

The rest rent equipment at margins that erase profit, and borrow from informal lenders at rates that erase the future. Capital, mechanisation, and information remain priced out of the small-farm economy.5

SOURCE · Agricultural Machinery Manufacturers Association · NABARD
18%

Of India's GDP comes from agriculture. 46% of the workforce depends on it.

This is not a niche problem. When the smallholder's economics break, the country's largest employer breaks. Every solvent farmer is a public good. Every insolvent one is a public liability.6

SOURCE · MoSPI · NSSO Labour Force Survey 2022
II Chapter Two — A Village Called Khimsepur FOL. 03

An ordinary village. Which is exactly the point.

We began our research in a single village — Khimsepur, in the Farrukhabad district of Uttar Pradesh — precisely because nothing about it is exceptional. It is one of thousands of similar villages whose pattern of distress is so common that it has stopped being noticed. If a model works here, it will work in nine villages out of ten in the Indo-Gangetic plain.

Why Khimsepur

An average that has nowhere else to fall.

The youth in Khimsepur is educated — but underemployed. The fields are productive — but unprofitable. The technology is available — but unowned. The schemes exist — but unclaimed. The village holds, in miniature, every contradiction of Indian agricultural policy of the last fifty years.

It is also a village where change is plausible. The youth are connected. The infrastructure is in place. The state schemes — PKVY, PMKSY, PM-Kisan — are operational. The only missing piece is a credible institutional vehicle. That is what we propose to build.

II.b An Iceberg Reading of Farmer Distress METHOD · SYSTEMS THINKING

Field interviews surface the visible. The Iceberg Model surfaces what they sit on. Without addressing the deepest layer — the mental models — any economic intervention will simply re-bake the same problem under a new name.

LAYER 01 · ABOVE WATEREvents
  • Low income among small farmers
  • Recurring agrarian distress and indebtedness
LAYER 02 · BELOW WATERLINEPatterns & Trends
  • Persistent poor production-to-cost ratio
  • Marketable surplus shrinks faster than input costs rise
  • Mandi prices systematically lower than consumer-end prices
LAYER 03 · STRUCTUREUnderlying Systems
  • Middlemen and APMC agents capturing most of the value
  • Fragmented and shrinking land holdings (1.08 ha avg)
  • Absent rural infrastructure: storage, cold chain, processing
  • Dependence on informal credit and seasonal moneylenders
LAYER 04 · DEEPESTMental Models
  • "Capitalism makes the rich richer and the poor poorer" — a sense of resignation
  • Pride in independent landownership makes cooperation feel like surrender
  • Discomfort with structural change — "this is how it has always been"
  • Distrust of government schemes after decades of leaky implementation

Project KISAAN is engineered to act on all four layers — not just the topmost.

IV Chapter Four — Three Knots to Untie FOL. 05

The crisis is one. But it has three distinct knots.

A successful intervention must untie all three at once. Solving any single knot leaves the system as exploitative as we found it. Project KISAAN is designed against all three simultaneously.

KNOT 01 · LAND

Small & fragmented holdings.

An individual farmer with 1–2 hectares cannot achieve economies of scale — on inputs, on equipment, on logistics, on bargaining. The only mathematical way out is to aggregate without dispossessing. Aggregate the buying power, not the deed.

KNOT 02 · INFORMATION

Unreliable, asymmetric information.

The farmer does not know yesterday's mandi price, this week's weather window, next month's scheme deadline, or the chemistry of the soil under his feet. Every information asymmetry becomes someone else's profit margin.

KNOT 03 · MARKET

An unbroken chain of middlemen.

Between the farmer's plough and the consumer's plate sit five to seven intermediaries. Each takes a margin. Each of those margins, in dairy, was eliminated — or formalised — by Amul. The blueprint exists. It just needs porting to grain.

V Chapter Five — A Proven Path FOL. 06

We are not inventing a model. We are porting one.

The cooperative is not theory in India. It is the single largest engine of rural prosperity the country has ever built. Four case studies make the case — one historical, one contemporary, two digital.

Founded in Anand, Gujarat, in 1946 by farmers tired of being underpaid by Polson Dairy. Today, the Gujarat Cooperative Milk Marketing Federation (GCMMF) is the world's strongest dairy brand. Three tiers: village societies, district unions, state federation. Democratic governance. Professional management. Production and marketing separated by design.

VI Chapter Six — The Proposal FOL. 07

Two levers, pulled together.

KISAAN proposes a single intervention with two interlocking components. Neither works alone. Together, they reinforce each other: the cooperative gives the farmer institutional weight; organic methods give the cooperative a defensible price premium and a long-term market.

LEVER 01 · STRUCTURAL

A Khimsepur Farmers' Cooperative.

Modelled on the Amul three-tier structure, scaled to a single village. Voluntary membership, one-member-one-vote governance, equitable profit-sharing, formal registration under the UP Co-operative Societies Act, 1965. The cooperative becomes the unit through which farmers buy inputs, share equipment, access credit, and sell produce.

  • Pools purchasing power for seeds, manure, and tractor time
  • Acquires formal credit at institutional interest rates
  • Negotiates directly with off-takers, removing middlemen
  • Holds, as a reserve, a percentage agreed by general body
  • Becomes the legal counterparty the government and banks can sign with
LEVER 02 · ECONOMIC & ECOLOGICAL

Organic methods, supported by PKVY.

Transition from chemical to organic over three years — using the financial support, training, and certification machinery built into the Government of India's Paramparagat Krishi Vikas Yojana. Cluster-based, with the cooperative as the cluster. Output earns a premium of 10–15% in domestic and export markets.3

  • ₹31,500 per hectare over three years, paid via DBT
  • Eligibility for Participatory Guarantee System (PGS) certification
  • Access to the Jaivik Kheti digital marketplace
  • Soil regeneration, reduced input costs after Year 2
  • Eligibility for India Organic and NPOP export certification
THE WHY · GOLDEN CIRCLE

At the centre is not the crop. It is the person.

Farmers are the backbone of the country and responsible for its food security. The indifference and disparagement they are treated with has led to disconcerting circumstances. They deserve due respect, and a better standard of living.
VII Chapter Seven — How It Works FOL. 08

A constitution. Five domains. One information board.

The cooperative is not a slogan. It is a registered legal entity with a written constitution, elected office-holders, regular general body meetings, and an audited reserve. Below is the operational anatomy.

Setup & Governance

i.

Membership & Voting

  • Open to all farmers & workers in the village
  • One person, one vote — no proxies
  • Prescribed maximum shareholding per member
  • Decisions on trade, inputs, head election by ⅔ majority
  • Regular general body meetings at member-set intervals
ii.

Registration

  • Co-operative Societies Act, 1912 / UP Co-op Act, 1965
  • Minimum 10 adult signatures to incorporate
  • Bye-laws submitted along with formation papers
  • Eligible from day one for PMKSY benefits
  • Eligible for PKVY cluster certification & subsidy
iii.

Finance

  • Revenue distributed equitably among members
  • A set percentage retained as general reserve
  • A second percentage allocated for community welfare
  • Audited annually; books open to all members
  • Access to KCC and crop insurance as a registered entity

Five Elected Domains

Beyond a general head and a meeting reporter, the cooperative elects domain leads — each accountable to the general body, each serving a one-year term renewable by ⅔ majority. New domains may be added as the cooperative scales.

DOMAIN 01

Information & Training

Relays policy updates, agricultural advisories, weather. Organises seminars, demonstrations, and skill workshops.

DOMAIN 02

Finance

Maintains books, sets seasonal budgets, manages reserve and welfare funds, prepares audit-ready accounts.

DOMAIN 03

Input Procurement

Procures certified seeds, manure, bio-fertilisers, and equipment at bulk rates. Maintains supplier roster.

DOMAIN 04

Trade

Identifies fair-price off-takers — FPOs, retail chains, e-commerce platforms, exporters. Negotiates contracts.

DOMAIN 05

Conflict Resolution

First-line mediator for member disputes. Maintains procedural fairness. Reports unresolved cases to general body.

A Physical Anchor

The Information Booth

A small cyber-corner at the cooperative office — a few computers, a chalk-and-paper information board, and a fixed phone line. Built deliberately as a place not an app, because the older generation of farmers does not transact through screens. Trust comes from a doorway, a chair, and a familiar face behind a desk.

  • Hosts the digital library of meeting minutes, policy updates, training records
  • Channel for lodging complaints, raising issues, applying for membership
  • Serves as the cooperative's day-to-day physical meeting spot
  • Has a single dial-in helpline — staffed by the elected booth manager
  • The information board is updated daily at sunrise

XI Chapter Eleven — Risks & Mitigations FOL. 12

No project this honest can be risk-free. Here is what could break it — and how we will hold the line.

RISK 01
The Mentality.
Decades of dependence on traders and pride in independent landholding mean cooperation feels foreign. Even with a credible model on the table, farmers may decline to join — a soft "no" that becomes a hard problem.
Mitigation We start with the youth (educated, less resistant), the locally-respected, and the families that have already lost members to migration. Membership grows by example, not exhortation.
RISK 02
Quantity over Quality.
The post-Green-Revolution farmer is trained to maximise yield by any means. The organic transition lowers yield by 15–25% in the first two years before recovering. Members may panic and revert.
Mitigation PKVY's ₹31,500/ha bridges the transition years. Premium pricing on certified output offsets volume loss. Group certification reduces individual cost. We publish the math openly at every general body meeting.
RISK 03
Contract Farming Trust.
Large off-takers offer assured revenue but historically exploit power asymmetry. Lack of contract transparency is a real risk — farmers have been burned before, and remember it.
Mitigation The cooperative, not the individual farmer, signs. Contract templates reviewed by NGO partners and pro-bono legal counsel. Any contract clause translated and read aloud at general body before signature.
RISK 04
Government Procedural Friction.
Scheme enrolment, certification, subsidy disbursement — each step has real-world delay and informal cost. Members lose interest if the promised support takes eighteen months.
Mitigation A dedicated field officer for the cooperative's first three years — whose only job is to chase paperwork. We treat bureaucracy as a budget line, not an afterthought.
RISK 05
Internal Corruption.
Any institution that handles money can be skimmed. A cooperative without robust controls becomes a private fiefdom of its elected head — the very problem it was created to solve.
Mitigation Public ledger, annual external audit, two-signatory disbursement, mandatory rotation of office every two terms, and a written grievance channel that bypasses the head.
RISK 06
Loan Repayment Failure.
Crop failure, monsoon variability, or a market dip in Year 1 could leave the cooperative unable to service institutional loans — collapsing the very credit relationship that gives it leverage.
Mitigation PMFBY crop insurance enrolment for all members from Day 1. A reserve fund built before the first harvest. Diversified cropping pattern. A reserve revolving credit line negotiated with the partner bank.
XII Chapter Twelve — The Roadmap FOL. 13

One village. Then ten. Then a hundred. A deliberate arc.

Prove the Khimsepur Model.
Months 1–18 · Khimsepur village, Farrukhabad

Onboard 30–50 founding farmer-members. Incorporate under the UP Co-operative Societies Act. Enrol in PKVY. Build the information booth. Hire a field officer. Sign first off-take contract by month 12. Publish a transparent Year-1 impact report.
